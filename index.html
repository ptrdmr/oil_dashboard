America's oil situation

Compiled April 5, 2026 — Sources: EIA, DOE, UANI, Lloyd's List, Bloomberg, CNBC

Tip: click a stockpile card, supply row, or price tile (pointer cursor) to open a chart of past snapshots from metric-history.json.

This dashboard tracks America's oil supply position during the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis. It shows how much crude oil the U.S. has in storage, where daily supply comes from, and how long reserves could last under different disruption scenarios.

The goal is to present a clear, at-a-glance picture of U.S. energy resilience — not to predict markets or recommend policy.

About the data
  • Stockpile figures (commercial crude, gasoline, distillates, Cushing) come from the EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report — an official U.S. government source updated each Wednesday.
  • Production and consumption estimates are drawn from the EIA Short-Term Energy Outlook.
  • SPR levels come from the Department of Energy.
  • Those government-sourced stockpile and supply figures are the most reliable numbers on the dashboard.
  • Hormuz transit counts, stranded tanker estimates, and attack tallies are compiled from maritime intelligence sources (UANI, Lloyd's List, Bloomberg) and news reporting — they are best-available estimates, not official counts, and may lag or vary between sources.
  • Oil prices reflect market data at the time of update.
  • The "days to empty" projections are simplified models, not forecasts; they assume constant draw rates and do not account for policy responses, demand shifts, or supply changes that would occur in practice.

Links to sources, with short descriptions of what each one is for, are at the bottom of the page.

    Shortage propagation: how the Gulf crisis reaches U.S. shores
    Each stage is a link in the chain. When Gulf-dependent buyers scramble, they outbid the cargoes the U.S. normally draws on.
    Why this matters: The U.S. gets only ~0.3 mb/d directly from the Persian Gulf, so a Hormuz closure looks small on the supply chart. But Asian and European refiners depend on it heavily. When they scramble for non-Gulf crude, they outbid U.S. Gulf Coast and Atlantic Basin buyers for the same cargoes — so the shortage reaches America through price and cargo competition, not through pipeline cuts. The further up the chain the red moves, the closer the squeeze gets to the U.S. dock.

    Reading this: Paper is the futures price quoted on TV — a financial contract traded on ICE. Physical (Dated Brent, assessed by Platts) is what actual North Sea cargoes trade at. When a chokepoint like Hormuz tightens, cargoes get scarce before futures reprice, so physical premiums blow out. A persistent $20+/bbl gap and a steep backwardation (front month above deferred months) both flash: supply is tight right now, even if the headline screens haven't fully caught up.

    Reading this chart

    What the chart is showing

    • Vertical axis (Y): Inventories in millions of barrels. The blue series is commercial crude stocks; the magenta series is the Strategic Petroleum Reserve. The dashed gray line is the sum of those two — total crude held in commercial and government storage, which is the relevant measure for overall cover.
    • Horizontal axis (X): Elapsed time from the present: “Now,” then monthly steps (M+4, M+8, …) out to 36 months (M+36). Each scenario uses a fixed daily shortfall and a fixed rule for how much of the draw comes from commercial stocks versus the SPR. The projection is intentionally simplified so scenarios can be compared side by side; it is an illustrative model, not a detailed market forecast.
    • The red dashed line: Think of it as a minimum comfortable cushion for total crude stocks (commercial + SPR). We drew it at about 90 days’ worth of oil using a rough, conservative guess at how much the U.S. still net-imports per day — the same idea as the old rule of thumb that big consuming countries should hold something like 90 days of import cover in emergency stocks. When the gray combined line drifts toward or under that red line, you’re in the zone where policymakers and analysts start to get nervous — not because the chart says something magical happens on that exact day, but because you’re no longer looking “fat and happy” on inventories. It’s a reference line for conversation, not a law of physics and not a promise about what Washington will do.
    • “Days to critical” in the boxes above the chart: A back-of-the-envelope: take all the oil you started with in this model and divide by the scenario’s daily shortfall. That gives you a single headline number for how long until stocks are effectively gone if nothing changed. It won’t line up to the month with the moment the gray line kisses the red one — for that story, watch the shape of the lines.

    Moderate disruption — where the numbers come from

    15% off the Canada pipeline: By early March 2026 Canada was already looking at a 10% tariff on energy exports to the U.S., and the trade fight was heating up. Cutting the flow 15% is our way of saying: tariffs make shipping oil south less attractive, and Alberta might squeeze supply on purpose in retaliation — they’ve done a version of that before with production curtailments. It’s meant to feel serious but not apocalyptic.

    30% less from the Atlantic basin: When tanker markets go haywire, the pain isn’t only at Hormuz. Picture 200+ ships tied up, insurance going through the roof, and fewer hulls to go around. Cargoes that used to head here from West Africa, Brazil, or Guyana start getting bid away by Asian buyers who just lost Hormuz barrels and will pay anything. 30% is our middle-of-the-road guess for how much of that Atlantic / Americas trade into the U.S. could get pulled away in a scramble like that.

    88% refinery runs: Real plants were running about 91.4% in mid-March and already sliding. Dropping to 88% keeps you inside the kind of “normal operating band” the EIA talks about; once you fall below that band, you’re usually into planned maintenance or something actually choking supply.

    Severe disruption — a stress test, not a forecast

    Half the Canadian pipe and no seaborne imports at all is the “everything breaks at once” story: tariffs pushed to something like an embargo, and every ocean route is too pricey or too risky to count on. We’re not saying that’s what will happen — we’re asking what it looks like if it did.
